    Activity: Using metadata in research practice

    You are planning a research project and need to identify and reuse existing data.

    A dataset looks relevant based on its title, but before using it, you need to decide whether it is suitable.

    Task 1: What metadata would you need?

    List the types of metadata you would want to check before deciding to use the dataset.

    Reveal example answer

    You might look for:

    • Creator / organisation (to assess credibility)
    • Data collection date (to check timeliness)
    • Geographic coverage (to see if it matches your study)
    • Variable names and descriptions (to understand what is measured)
    • File format (to ensure you can use the data)
    • Licensing / access conditions (to confirm reuse is allowed)

    Task 2: Why does this metadata matter?

    Pick two types of metadata and explain how they help you decide whether the data is suitable.

    Reveal example answer
    • Geographic coverage ensures the data is relevant to your population of interest
    • Licensing conditions determine whether you are allowed to reuse the data

    Task 3: Identify potential challenges

    What problems might you face if key metadata is missing or unclear?

    Reveal example answer
    • You may not understand what variables represent
    • You may not know if the data is reliable or trustworthy
    • You may be unable to reuse the data due to unclear licensing
    • You could waste time analysing unsuitable data

    Task 4: Reflect on your own research

    Think about your own work or a project you are familiar with.

    What metadata would be most important for you, and why?

    Reveal example answer

    Answers will vary, but should connect metadata to specific research needs (e.g. variables for analysis, provenance for trust, formats for usability).

    Task 5: Evaluate metadata quality

    What makes metadata “good quality”?

    List 2–3 characteristics you would expect.

    Reveal example answer
    • Clear and precise descriptions
    • Use of standardised terms
    • Complete and consistent fields
    • Sufficient detail for understanding and reuse
